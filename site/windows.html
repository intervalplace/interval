A window is not the world

The world is not in either of these pages. It is a protocol: a rule for turning one state and a set of deeds into the next state, agreed six hundred milliseconds at a time by every node that runs it. Interval is that agreement. Everything you see here is one program's opinion about how to paint it.

Which means these two are not the client and the alternative client. They are two windows, written first, out of the same protocol anyone else can read. A window that draws the world in a terminal, or reads it aloud, or paints it on a headset, or fits it into a watch face, is not a lesser way to play Interval. It is Interval, seen from somewhere else.

What the world insists on

A window may choose its colours, its camera, its sounds, whether to show a map at all. It may not choose what is true. Where a citizen stands, what they carry, which country they are in, how much of the world they have touched: all of it is settled by the protocol before any window opens, and every window that reads the same tick must agree about every one of them.

That line is drawn deliberately, and it is drawn tightly. Two people who disagree about where the Fens end cannot arrange to meet there, so geography is law. Two people whose windows report different levels for the same citizen do not have a disagreement about pixels, they have a disagreement about a person, so standing and calling are law. Almost nothing else is.

Write your own

The state of the world is public and readable over a plain HTTP endpoint, and a window needs nothing more than that plus the ability to sign a deed with a key. No permission, no registration, no approved client list. If your hashes match, you are a citizen, and so is whatever you wrote to look at it with.

The manual documents the state and the rules. The New Player Guide gets you playing in ten minutes. Both windows above ship with the node, and both are yours to take apart.
